RMS Titanic

Charles Joughin, who had carefully secured his lifejacket before entering the sea, outsmarted the North Atlantic's sharpest and coldest weapon. Stephen Cheung, a hypothermia expert from Brock University, commented that although alcohol isn’t your usual hack for survival, it suprisingly gave Joughin a bigger brain. It may have reduced any sensations of lower tempuratures to be absorbed through his skin, reducing his anxiety levels. Unintentionally, Joughin met several key principles for surviving a maritime disaster. First, he avoided the freezing waters as long as possible—he was one of the very last people to get wet that night. Second, and most critically, he maintained his head above the water and succesfully got out.

Joughin floated in the darkness for close to two hours. As the sun began to rise, he spotted a defeated lifeboat. He swam to it and pulled himself aboard.

RMS Empress of Ireland

The family was suddenly thrown out of their sleep by the sound of what Grace described as a firecracker. A person eventually came rushing into their cabin to warn them of the ship's listing and to get into the lifeboats as soon as possible. The family could not take advantage of the stairs nearby because the ship was flipping faster than they could climb. Luckily, they reached the top of the stairs and sat on the railings beside the Evans family until the ship had completely turned over to its side and disappeared under the water.

Grace got submerged under the water twice before she secured her grip to a piece of wreckage floating about. She could not distinguish her parents among the crowd and the constant screaming and splashing. Grace, catching the attention of a few lifeboats nearby, was rescued by a crowded lifeboat and fell unconscious. She regained her senses on the Storstad, where she found herself in bed without her parents, Edward James and Edith Emily.

MV Wihelm Gustloff

Rose Petrus (formerly Rezas) is originally from Schwarzort—now known as Juodkrantė, Lithuania—a small village of about 400 people. At the time, she was working in Gotenhafen (modern-day Gdynia). As the Russian forces advanced rapidly, there was no escape except across the freezing waters of the Baltic Sea.

Eventually, Petrus fell unconscious. When she opened her eyes once again, she found herself in what looked like a man’s naval uniform. Her legs were swollen; knees and hands felt inflated like a water balloon, and the discomfort of not being able to stretch her feet could not be put into words—they were stiff and frozen from being stranded in the icy water for so long. She greatly missed the feeling of being warm, and in that moment, she believed she had taken all those moments for granted until someone brought in whiskey to consume till their satisfaction. It was four o’clock in the morning. The ship that rescued us, a German destroyer, was already packed with refugees.

MV Dona Paz

With another explosion, the fire reached the surrounding waters, trapping the Dona Paz in a ring of fire due to the gasoline and kerosene spilling onto the sea where it inconveniently sat on the surface. Many survivors had no choice but to dive under the boiling water.

Elutia Bascal heard the muffled groans of her father, Salvador Bascal, an experienced diver and fisherman. She called back to him, and soon they were able to locate each other’s voice among the flaming waters. When they were close enough, Salvador grabbed Elutia’s hand, but his hand slipped, accidentally ripping a piece of her burnt skin off with him. They both swam for more than an hour, seeking rescue. The only military soldier, Luthgardo Niedo, who escaped the tragedy believed his fellow soldiers were either burned alive, drowned, or eaten by sharks underneath the burns of the flames.
